# Scaling Categorical Flow Maps 

Oscar Davis ∗, Anastasiia Filippova , Pierre Ablin , Victor Turrisi , Amitis Shidani , Marco Cuturi , Louis Béthune 

Apple Continuous diffusion and flow matching models could represent a powerful alternative to autoregressive approaches for language modelling (LM), as they unlock a host of advantages currently reserved for continuous modalities, including accelerated sampling and tilting. Recently, several works have demonstrated the possibility of generating discrete data continuously by a simple flow matching process between a Gaussian and the one-hot encoded data distribution. They have further shown the feasibility of accelerated sampling via Categorical Flow Maps (CFMs), resulting in competitive sample quality in the few-step regime. However, this method had only been evaluated at relatively modest scales ( < 1B), leaving the question of its scalability completely open. In this article, we train a 1.7B-parameter base flow model on 2.1Ttokens and self-distill it into a CFM that generates diverse, high-quality text in as few as 4 inference steps while maintaining near-data-level token entropy. Furthermore, we introduce a likelihood bound for CFMs in the semi-discrete setting, and show that they can be used to score the model on standard LM benchmarks, achieving results in the same range as discrete diffusion methods. Finally, we uncover some of the challenges that arise from training these models at scale, and we provide prescriptive insights on loss weighting and time scheduling.

# 1 Introduction 

Although in language modelling the dominant paradigm has remained autoregressive (AR), diffusion mod-els (Sohl-Dickstein et al., 2015; Ho et al., 2020) and flow matching (Lipman et al., 2023; Albergo et al., 2023) were responsible for large breakthroughs on continuous modalities. Driven by their success, a line of work has sought to extend diffusion to discrete distributions, an approach coined discrete diffusion (Austin et al., 2021; Hoogeboom et al., 2021; Campbell et al., 2022; Sahoo et al., 2024; Shi et al., 2024; Gat et al., 2024). The hope of these models was to extend the rich and principled diffusion toolbox to discrete data, includ-ing guidance (Ho and Salimans, 2022), distribution tilting (Domingo-Enrich et al., 2025), and accelerated sampling (Song et al., 2023; Song and Dhariwal, 2023). However, discrete diffusion methods evolve on the discrete grid, preventing the usage of the well-established continuous techniques. A parallel line of work developed continuous-state processes to generate discrete data, either on the sim-plex (Stark et al., 2024; Davis et al., 2024; Cheng et al., 2024) or directly in Rd (Eijkelboom et al., 2025). Recently, Roos et al. (2026); Lee et al. (2026); Potaptchik et al. (2026b) scaled these ideas to common discrete diffusion benchmarks, and showed that these methods are competitive, but also succeeded in porting flow map matching (Boffi et al., 2025a,b) to discrete data, enabling high quality few-step sampling. We refer to these methods as Categorical Flow Maps (CFMs). If successful at scale, CFMs could unlock the flow map toolbox for steering and fine-tuning (Holderrieth et al., 2026; Potaptchik et al., 2026a), trading compute for sample quality rather than merely generating plausible outputs. Nonetheless, it remains unclear whether CFMs can scale to the billion-parameter, trillion-token regime. For a token vocabulary V , and context length L, they require materialising L × | V | logit matrices, where |V | can easily attain 200 ,000 tokens in modern LLMs, imposing a significant memory and computational overhead. In this work, we show that CFMs scale competitively to this setting. Our contributions are as follows:

Current best-performing approaches for CFMs rely on a two-stage training (Lee et al., 2026; Potaptchik et al., 2026b): in the first stage, a simple cross-entropy denoiser is trained (see equation (2.10)); in the second stage, that denoiser is self-distilled into a CFM (see equations (2.11) and (2.12)). The two-stage approach is necessary, because the self-distillation objectives rely on a biased signal from the model itself, so ap-plying them from the start degrades final performance. More-over, self-distillation objectives are computationally demand-ing (requiring multiple forward passes per gradient step), making their optimisation over the entire training trajectory prohibitively expensive. In this section, we investigate the importance of three com-ponents in the design space of CFM pretraining: the time schedule mixture, the adaptive loss weight, and the random “unmasking” of inputs. 3.1 Design space of CFMs  

> Time scheduling.

Lee et al. (2026); Potaptchik et al. (2026b) show the importance of the time schedule for CFMs. Instead of the linear interpolant schedule, a monotone map γ : [0 , 1] → [0 , 1] , coined the “error decoding” schedule (Sahoo et al., 2025; Pynadath et al., 2025), significantly improves the final model’s performance. This schedule adapts the time so that the probability of incorrectly decoding the current point, 

P (argmax It̸ = argmax I1), grows linearly in t, instead of exhibiting a sharp phase transition of the linear schedule. They report that a convex combination of it with the linear schedule, αt = (1 − λ)t + λγ t, further improves the generation quality. While Potaptchik et al. (2026b) train with λ = 0 .9, Lee et al. (2026) use it only at inference with λ ∈ { 0.5, 0.75 , 1}. We ablate over this mixture choice.  

> Adaptive loss weighting.

Potaptchik et al. (2026b) adopt the adaptive loss weight of Geng et al. (2024): for any objective of the form KL( p ∥ q), they compute ∆( x) = ∥p(x) − q(x)∥22, and reweight by w(x) = sg ((∆( x) + c)−r ), with constants c = 10 −2 and r = 1 /2 on the diagonal loss. The resulting objective is

> Random unmasking of input sequence

The models are by default only trained on generating full, unconditional sequences, but final downstream applications typically require conditional generation given prompts. To match this setting, we follow Dieleman et al. (2022), and with a probability p unmask a random prefix of the text, sampled from U[1 , L/d ], with d | L. We vary both p and d.

3.2 Hyperparameter sweep  

> Setup.

We train a 400 M-parameter adaptation of diffusion transformers from SEDD (Lou et al., 2023), which is also used in Roos et al. (2026); Lee et al. (2026). In particular, we use the transformer from Roos et al. (2026), which follow the recommendations of Zhou et al. (2025) to upper-bound the Lipschitz constant of the model for more stable training. We train over 16 B tokens, resulting in about 40 tokens per parameter over the Nemotron pre-training dataset (Basant et al., 2025) using the TikToken tokenizer (OpenAI, 2023). As in Nie et al. (2025), we also randomly crop sequences to ensure the models have been trained on different context lengths. > Results.

Figure 4 summarises our first sweep over a total of 30 configurations. Adaptive loss weighting (r = 0 .5) uniformly improves Gen PPL but slightly degrades MAUVE ⋆, revealing a trade-off between token-level quality and distributional fidelity. Since MAUVE ⋆ is upper-bounded by 1, the Gen PPL gain is more practically meaningful than the MAUVE ⋆ degradation at these values. We confirm this in Section 4, where MAUVE ⋆ reaches near-maximal values (about 0.94 ) despite having the adaptive loss weight enabled. The schedule optimum lies near λ = 0 .75 , with a sharp collapse at λ = 1 , which shows that pure error-decoding without mixing is detrimental. Clean prefix probability p > 0 is essential for MAUVE ⋆: without it, scores remain near zero regardless of other settings, resulting in out-of-distribution outputs. In a follow-up grid of 324 configurations (Figure 5), we narrow the ranges around these best settings and find that Gen PPL and MAUVE ⋆ are primarily sensitive to learning rate and weight decay, while p, d, and λ are robust in the selected range. 

3.3 Hyperparameter transfer 

Searching for optimal hyperparameters directly at large scale is expensive. Therefore, it is common to rely on zero-shot hyperparameter transfer rules to identify optimal hyperparameters when scaling the model size, batch size, and token horizon (Yang et al., 2022; Dey et al., 2026; Mlodozeniec et al., 2025). We attempted to leverage Complete(d)P (Mlodozeniec et al., 2025) by performing a hyperparameter sweep on a 400 M model; see Appendix D.3. Since our architecture includes time-conditioning layers that are not covered by existing transfer theories, we adapted the method by treating these layers as hidden layers. However, our experiments in Appendix D.4 show that the baseline approach, with neither depth nor width transfer, achieved the best performance. We therefore leave zero-shot hyperparameter transfer for CFMs as an open question.

> 4.2.2 Full self-distillation

The results of the full-scale, 200 k steps self-distillation are available in Figure 1 and Figure 8. Both flow-maps provide samples substantially closer to the data distribution in terms of token entropy. Moreover, while the undistilled model produced degenerate samples in the 1 – 2 step regime, which artificially lowers the Gen PPL, the flow map models generate diverse samples, though Gen PPL remains high. The MAUVE ⋆ is improved by both models in the few-step regime, with the most prominent improvement at 4 sampling steps. The JSD overall seems to slightly degrade generation quality; we hypothesise this is due to the higher token entropy it produces compared to forward KL. We additionally report Delta Perplexity (∆ PPL), which measures a model’s ability to complete text condi-tioned on a dataset prompt. Given a prompt from the dataset, the evaluated model generates the remaining text, and we report the perplexity of the generated completion under Qwen 2.5 7B (Yang et al., 2025). De-picted in Figure 9, the ∆ PPL of the self-distilled models improves significantly in the few-step regime, while recovering similar perplexities for more inference steps.

# 5 Related work  

> Continuous diffusion for discrete data.

Early work by Dieleman et al. (2022) attempted to learn the embeddings of the discrete data using the reparametrisation trick; Li et al. (2022) explored a similar approach with word embeddings. Later, Stark et al. (2024) designed an interpolation scheme directly on the simplex. Davis et al. (2024); Cheng et al. (2024) leveraged Riemannian geometry to alleviate the issues of linear interpolants. Variational Flow Matching (Eijkelboom et al., 2025) shows how to flow from a Gaussian to one-hot encoded data. CFM (Roos et al., 2026), FMLM (Lee et al., 2026), and DFM (Potaptchik et al., 2026b) adopted this approach, and reached competitive performance with discrete diffusion baselines. Our work builds directly on this line, and demonstrates its scalability to the 1.7B parameter regime.  

> Accelerated sampling.

Consistency models (Song et al., 2023; Song and Dhariwal, 2023) pioneered the idea of mapping any point along a diffusion trajectory to the trajectory’s endpoint, enabling one- or few-step 9generation. Flow map matching (Boffi et al., 2025a,b; Geng et al., 2025) provides a principled framework for learning such maps via self-distillation of a pretrained flow. CFMs (Roos et al., 2026; Lee et al., 2026; Potaptchik et al., 2026b) port flow map matching to the semi-discrete setting, reducing inference to a single forward pass while retaining the continuous-state benefits. Other related works are discussed in Appendix A. 

# 6 Limitations 

CFMs require materialising full L × | V | continuous-state trajectory matrices for all steps. At our scale (L = 2048 , |V | ≈ 100 k), this is manageable given sufficient hardware ( 256 H100s), but the cost grows linearly in context length and vocabulary size. We absorb this bottleneck through compute and efficient cross-entropy implementation (Wijmans et al., 2025), without solving it. Moreover, CFMs also present challenges in principled hyperparameter transfer. Unlike standard transformers, CFMs condition on continuous times via dedicated layers, which fall outside the parametrisations covered by Complete(d)P (Mlodozeniec et al., 2025). Our attempts to apply Complete(d)P to this architecture resulted in worse performance than direct tuning at the target scale. Adapting time-conditioned variants of μP (Zheng et al., 2025) is left for future work. 

# 7 Conclusion 

We have shown that Categorical Flow Maps scale competitively to the 1.7B-parameter regime. After self-distillation, they produce diverse, high-quality text in as few as 4 function evaluations while maintaining near-data-level token entropy, pushing the quality vs. throughput Pareto front beyond discrete diffusion baselines. Our semi-discrete ELBO places the model in the same ballpark as other non-autoregressive methods, though a gap remains with approaches trained directly on ELBO maximisation. These results suggest that continuous-state generation is a viable path for discrete data at LLM scale, and that the broader flow map toolbox (guidance, tilting, reward fine-tuning) is now within reach for language modelling.

12 A Related work  

> Continuous diffusion for discrete data.

With a different approach, Chen et al. (2026) proposed a way of learning continuous trajectories for discrete data, leveraging embedding spaces and Bregman divergences.  

> Discrete diffusion.

Diffusion models for discrete state spaces originate from D3PM (Austin et al., 2021) and multinomial diffusion (Hoogeboom et al., 2021), which design discrete forward processes whose reversal can be learned by a neural network. Campbell et al. (2022) recast the framework in continuous time and consider the rate transition matrices of the reverse CTMC. SEDD (Lou et al., 2023) replaces the standard ELBO with a score-entropy objective, improving both training stability and sample quality. Masked diffusion (Sahoo et al., 2024; Shi et al., 2024) simplified the earlier approaches, resulting in a simple cross-entropy objective that forms an ELBO on the data distribution. Discrete Flow Matching (Gat et al., 2024) generalised the framework by learning a probability velocity field over discrete states. (Schiff et al., 2024) further investigated the uniform-state case of discrete diffusion.  

> Accelerated sampling for discrete diffusion.

Several approaches have attempted to implement accelerated meth-ods for discrete diffusion. Sahoo et al. (2025) shows how a continuous Gaussian latent can generate a uniform-state discrete diffusion process, enabling consistent trajectories for SDTT-like self-distillation (Deschenaux and Gulcehre, 2025). Hayakawa et al. (2025) show that by incorporating correlation through diffusion steps the few-step sampling regime can be improved. Based on similar observations, Yoo et al. (2025) propose to refine the couplings between source and target distributions.

The backbone is a transformer with AdaLN-Zero modulation (Peebles and Xie, 2023), rotary position em-beddings (Su et al., 2023), SwiGLU MLP, and RMSNorm, following the Lipschitz recommendations of Zhou et al. For s = t, the model is a standard denoiser; for s̸ = t, the two conditionings allow learning the full two-time flow map.
