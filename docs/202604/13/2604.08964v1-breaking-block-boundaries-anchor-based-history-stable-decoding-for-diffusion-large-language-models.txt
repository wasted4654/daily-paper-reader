Abstract 

Diffusion Large Language Models (dLLMs) have recently become a promising alternative to autoregressive large language models (ARMs). Semi-autoregressive (Semi-AR) decoding is widely employed in base dLLMs and advanced decoding strategies due to its superior perfor-mance. However, our observations reveal that Semi-AR decoding suffers from inherent block constraints, which cause the decoding of many cross-block stable tokens to be unnecessarily delayed. To address this challenge, we system-atically investigate the identification of stable tokens and present three key findings: (1) naive lookahead decoding is unreliable, (2) token sta-bility closely correlates with convergence trend, and (3) historical information is isolated. Build-ing on these insights, we propose Anchor-based 

History-stable Decoding ( AHD ), a training-free, plug-and-play dynamic decoding strategy. Specifically, AHD monitors the stability trend of tokens in real time through dynamic anchors. Once a token reaches stability, it initiates early cross-block decoding to enhance efficiency and performance. Extensive experiments across lan-guage, vision-language, and audio-language do-mains demonstrate that AHD simultaneously improves both performance and inference effi-ciency. Notably, AHD effectively reverses the performance degradation typically observed in existing advanced decoding acceleration strate-gies. For instance, on the BBH benchmark, our approach reduces decoding steps by 80% while improving performance by 3.67% .

1 Introduction 

Recently, diffusion large language models (dLLMs) have attracted extensive attention from the com-munity and become a strong alternative to au-toregressive large language models (ARMs) (Nie et al., 2025; Ye et al., 2025). In contrast to the next -token prediction mechanism employed by

ARMs, dLLMs utilize bidirectional attention and iterative decoding, facilitating efficient parallel de-coding, flexible generation order, and controllable output (Berglund et al., 2024). In recent work, semi - autoregressive (Semi - AR) decoding has been widely adopted in base dLLMs (Yang et al., 2025; Nie et al., 2025; Zhu et al., 2025; Bie et al., 2025), and numerous advanced decoding strategies have been developed based on this framework (Wei et al., 2026; Wang et al., 2025a; Dong et al., 2025; Wu et al., 2025; Ma et al., 2025b; Wang et al., 2025b). However, Semi - AR decoding requires the com-pletion of the current block prior to initiating the subsequent one, thereby limiting the potential for parallel decoding. To investigate this constraint, we conducted a systematic study and obtained the following key observations: (i) Block - Boundary Delay is Prevalent. Specifically, Figure 1 (a) and (b) reveal that many tokens already match the fi-nal output before their corresponding blocks are decoded and maintain stability until they are even-tually unmasked. We define such tokens as cross-block stable tokens. However, the sequential nature of block decoding introduces unnecessary delays for these stable tokens, wasting many decoding steps. (ii) Cross - block Stable Tokens Exhibit Ra-diative Effects. Figure 1 (c) illustrates that the initial stable state of a token often emerges in a clustered pattern (i.e., the first stable step). This in-dicates that once a token reaches stability, it exerts an influence on neighboring tokens, accelerating their transition into stable states. Conversely, delay-ing the decoding of a stable token suppresses the entire local region, leading to performance degra-dation (Lu et al., 2026; Wang et al., 2025b). Therefore, we urgently need to free these stable tokens from the sequential block constraint, which is crucial for both inference efficiency and perfor-mance. This raises a key question: how can we accurately identify cross - block stable tokens? Pre-vious studies have often relied on metrics such as

confidence and entropy to determine whether a to-ken should be decoded (Nie et al., 2025; Wu et al., 2025; Ben-Hamu et al., 2025). Therefore, we sys-tematically examine the evolution of the confidence metric for cross - block stable tokens during decod-ing, and identify two critical insights: (I) Naive Lookahead Decoding is unreliable. As shown in Figure 2 (a), even these tokens that have already reached stability may still experience local fluctua-tions in subsequent decoding steps. These tempo-rary variations can potentially result in erroneous judgments. (II) Token stability closely correlates with convergence trend. As shown in Figure 2 (b), after the first stable step, the confidence trend of these stable tokens changes sharply, exhibiting a rapid growth pattern that we define as the absolute stability trend . This motivates us to redefine token stability in terms of the trajectory across decoding steps. Furthermore, it is more critical to capture the emergence of the absolute stability trend at an early stage than solely monitoring peak values. If tokens are unlocked only when their confidence exceeds a high threshold, the decoding process is forced to wait for many unnecessary steps and could exhibit the phenomenon outlined in insight I .Based on the above insights, we identify two essential conditions for the precise detection of cross-block token stability: (1) the formulation of a global stability metric that transcends local behav-ior; and (2) the capability to dynamically monitor trajectory information. To meet these conditions effectively, the integration of historical information is indispensable. However, by examining the de-coding characteristics of dLLMs, we find that: (III) Historical information is isolated in standard de-coding. In conventional iterative decoding within dLLMs, each prediction only depends on the out-come of the immediately preceding step. The final output is determined exclusively by the last decod-ing step, with no consideration of earlier iterations. Therefore, we argue that breaking this historical isolation by integrating trajectory information is essential for identifying these stable tokens. In response to these insights, we propose 

Anchor - based History -stable Decoding ( AHD ), a training - free, plug - and -play dynamic decoding ap-proach for monitoring cross - block stability. Specif-ically, at each decoding step, we take the current step as a dynamic anchor and retrospectively exam-ine prior steps in the historical buffer. Through this process, we establish a stability criterion grounded in historical consistency. This allows us to cap-ture the absolute stability trend at an early stage, avoiding additional decoding steps, and improv-ing robustness to local fluctuations. Moreover, the early unlocking of these cross-block stable tokens preserves the coherence of the decoding, thereby enhancing generation quality. Our contributions are summarized as follows: • We systematically analyze the key limitations of Semi - AR decoding and derive three critical insights, which serves as the motivation and key evidence for determining cross-block stability. • Based on the analysis, we propose AHD, a novel cross -block stability decoding method. It uses dy-namic anchors to trace back historical trajectories and capture absolute stability trends, enabling early unlocking of cross-block stable tokens. We also provide theoretical proof of the method. • Extensive experiments on language, vision-language, and audio-language domins show that AHD achieves consistent improvements in both performance and inference efficiency, signifi-cantly surpassing previous decoding strategies.

Semi-autoregressive Decoding. To further en-hance quality and controllability, the Semi - AR decoding has been widely adopted in base dLLMs (Nie et al., 2025; Yang et al., 2025; Zhu et al., 2025; Wang et al., 2025c; Xin et al., 2025). Recent advances in decoding algorithms often build upon this paradigm (Wu et al., 2025; Dong et al., 2025; Wang et al., 2025a; Wei et al., 2026; Kong et al., 2025; Kim et al., 2026; Li et al., 2026; Ma et al., 2025b). Its core principle is to partition the output sequence into multiple blocks and decode them sequentially from left to right. Within each block, the aforementioned diffusion decoding strat-egy is applied. Although this method constrains the generation order, it consistently achieves superior performance across various tasks. 

3 Method 

3.1 Key Limitation of Semi-AR Decoding 

Semi-AR decoding has been widely adopted across various base dLLMs and advanced decoding strate-gies. However, its architecture supports parallel generation only within individual blocks, while re-quiring sequential processing across blocks. This structural constraint inherently limits the parallel decoding potential of dLLMs. To address this, we first conducted a systematic analysis of Semi - AR decoding and identified several key observations, which serve as the motivation and foundation of our proposed method. 

Core observation 1: Block-boundary Delay is Prevalent. First, we conducted experiments us-ing LLaDA - 8B - Instruct (Nie et al., 2025) on a representative sample from GSM8K (Cobbe et al., 2021). As shown in Figure 1 (a), we observe that many tokens already match their final predicted val-ues before their corresponding blocks are decoded, and these tokens remain unchanged until eventually being unmasked. We refer to such tokens as cross-block stable tokens . To further quantify this phe-nomenon, we further evaluated three widely-used benchmarks (Chen et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2024b; Cobbe et al., 2021). As shown in Figure 1 (b), all three benchmarks contain many tokens whose de-coding is delayed by block boundaries, resulting in significant waste of decoding steps. 

Core observation 2: Cross-block Stable Tokens Exhibit Radiative Effects. As shown in Figure 1 (c), we further visualize the temporal and spatial distribution of the first stable step (marked as stars in Figure 1 (a)). It is defined as the earliest de-coding step at which a token reaches stability. We observe that the onset of stable tokens is not ran-domly dispersed but instead forms noticeable clus-ters. Specifically, once a token achieves stabil-ity, adjacent tokens tend to stabilize in subsequent steps. We interpret these findings as follows: dur-ing iterative decoding, stable tokens function as semantic anchors, exerting radiative and attractive influences on surrounding tokens. The delayed de-coding of these stable tokens suggests that block boundaries disrupt local inference within the block. 

3.2 Prerequisites for Cross-Block Decoding 

Based on above analysis, it is evident that Semi - AR decoding incurs significant block -boundary delays. To address this, a natural objective is to acceler-ate the release of stable tokens, which are delayed during decoding, thereby reducing overall decod-ing steps and improving inference coherence. This raises a key question: How can cross-block stable tokens be effectively and accurately identified? 

Core insight 1: Naive Lookahead Decoding is Unreliable. Previous studies have frequently em-(a) Local F luctuation

ployed confidence and entropy as decoding indi-cators (Wu et al., 2025; Ben-Hamu et al., 2025). Here, we utilize the confidence metric as stability measures for cross-block unlocking, and track the evolution of it. As shown in Figure 2 (a), our obser-vation reveals that stable tokens can still experience brief, large fluctuations in confidence metric even after their first stable step. These low-frequency fluctuations are critical, as they can induce misjudg-ments, and propagate erroneous signals to subse-quent iterations, ultimately affecting the quality of the final output. Moreover, both confidence and entropy metrics are computed based on a single de-coding step, rendering them particularly sensitive to local fluctuations. 

Core insight 2: Token Stability Closely Corre-lates with Convergence Trend. In addition to lo-cal fluctuations, we also identify another common and critical phenomenon. As illustrated in Figure 2 (b), the confidence curves for cross-block stable tokens typically remain persistently low or oscil-latory prior to the first stable step, indicating that the position is still engaged in iterative inference. However, after exceeding this step, the curves enter a distinct “ convergence phase ”, characterized by a sharp and monotonic increase in confidence. We term this phenomenon the absolute stability trend .If a token remains unlocked until its confidence exceeds an extremely high threshold, this leads to a significant increase in decoding steps and could exhibit the phenomenon outlined in insight 1 . To address this, we redefine token stability based on its confidence trajectory throughout the decoding process. Specifically, our approach is to identify and capture the trajectory at the earliest stage when it exhibits an absolutely stable trend. 

Core insight 3: Historical Information is Iso-lated in Standard Decoding. Building upon above insights, we conclude that two essential con-ditions must be satisfied to effectively and effi-ciently detect token stability: (1) the adoption of a global stability metric that transcends single-step evaluation, and (2) the ability to dynamically mon-itor information and capture the absolute stability trend. To satisfy these conditions, a crucial consid-eration is the incorporation of historical informa-tion. Through analysis of the standard decoding in dLLMs, we observe that each inference step oper-ates independently, disregarding previous predic-tions. Consequently, the final output is completely determined by the prediction pθ(x0 | x1) at the last step, while all intermediate predictive distribu-tions during decoding are ignored. Therefore, it is essential to break this inherent characteristic and leverage historical trajectory information.

4.2 Main Results Language Domain. We present the evaluation results for LLaDA-8B-Instruct and LLaDA-1.5 across seven benchmarks in Tables 1 and 2, compar-ing baseline approaches, recent advanced decoding strategies, and our proposed AHD. Overall, our method consistently surpasses both naive decod-ing and existing advanced strategies in terms of both performance and inference efficiency. No-tably, most advanced decoding methods achieve inference acceleration at the expense of model per-formance. Specifically, in rare cases where perfor-mance does improve, the gains are marginal (typi-cally within 1%), falling within the expected varia-tion and not reflecting a significant enhancement. In contrast, our method achieves consistent perfor-mance improvements across all seven benchmarks while simultaneously accelerating inference. For instance, on the BBH benchmark, LLaDA achieves a performance gain of +3.67 points while reducing decoding steps by 80%.

5 Conclusion 

In this work, we propose Anchor-based History-stable Decoding (AHD). Its core idea is to use dy-namic anchors to monitor the historical trajectory in real time, capture the absolute stability trend at an early stage, and thereby unlock stable tokens across blocks in advance. AHD substantially re-duces the decoding steps while consistently im-proving performance across multiple benchmarks in the language, multimodal, and audio domains. This offers a novel perspective for the development of future advanced decoding strategies. 

Limitations 

In this section, we discuss several limitations of our work. (1) Although AHD is a training - free, plug - and -play method, its parameters vary slightly across tasks, which increases the adaptation cost for new scenarios. (2) Our experiments mainly fo-cus on models of around 8B parameters, and have not yet validated effectiveness on larger models such as 72B or 256B. With more computational resources, we plan to explore longer sequences and larger - scale models. (3) Although the additional computation introduced by AHD is almost negli-gible, we further optimize the related operations, such as vectorization, to further improve inference efficiency.

A Related Work 

Diffusion Large Language Model. Diffusion models have achieved widespread success in image and video generation (Ho et al., 2020; Rombach et al., 2022; Melnik et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2023), inspiring growing interest in ap-plying them to discrete text domains. Migrating dif-fusion models from continuous to discrete domains presents unique challenges, and researchers have explored several technical approaches (Campbell et al., 2022; Lovelace et al., 2023; Yu et al., 2025a). Among these, Masked Diffusion Models (MDMs) have become the most mainstream branch (Austin et al., 2021a; Sahoo et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2023; Gong et al., 2025). This paradigm models text gen-eration as an iterative “mask–denoise” process. In the forward process, noise is introduced by grad-ually replacing tokens in the text with a special 

[MASK] absorption state. In the reverse process, the model learns to reconstruct the original text from a fully masked sequence (Ou et al., 2026; Sa-hoo et al., 2024; Shi et al., 2024; Nie et al., 2025). Inspired by this framework, researchers have suc-cessfully extended MDMs to diffusion large lan-guage models (dLLMs), with LLaDA (Nie et al., 2025) and Dream (Ye et al., 2025) attracting wide attention in the community. Their performance is competitive with autoregressive models with simi-lar scale (Yu et al., 2025a). 

Parallel Decoding. In dLLMs, logits for all to-ken positions are computed during each decoding iteration. Fast-dLLM identifies the root cause of degraded generation quality in parallel decoding as the violation of token dependencies under the con-ditional independence assumption. To address this, Fast-dLLM and Dimple propose confidence-aware parallel decoding (Wu et al., 2025; Yu et al., 2025b), which dynamically adjusts the number of tokens to unmask in each iteration based on a confidence threshold. This allows generating multiple tokens simultaneously while mitigating dependency vio-lations and preserving generation quality, thereby achieving a trade-off between quality and inference efficiency. 

Advanced Decoding Strategies for dLLM. Due to the iterative denoising nature of dLLMs, their in-ference latency is relatively high (Nie et al., 2025). Furthermore, as the attention in dLLMs is bidi-rectional, traditional KV caching is not directly applicable (Wu et al., 2025). This has led to the development of various acceleration strategies, in-cluding specialized KV cache mechanisms (Chen et al., 2025b; Liu et al., 2025; Ma et al., 2025a; Wu et al., 2025; Song et al., 2025; Huang et al., 2025a) and advanced decoding methods (Wu et al., 2025; Dong et al., 2025; Wang et al., 2025a; Wei et al., 2026; Kong et al., 2025; Kim et al., 2026; Li et al., 2026; Lu et al., 2026). Parallel decoding strategies typically sample multiple tokens at each step, thereby reducing the total number of iterative steps without retraining and achieving faster gen-eration. Early work such as Fast-dLLM (Wu et al., 2025) proposed a parallel decoding scheme based on a fixed confidence threshold. Saber (Dong et al., 2025) introduced adaptive acceleration by dynam-ically adjusting the parallel size and employed a backtracking re-masking mechanism to correct pre-diction errors, improving both speed and quality. WINO (Hong et al., 2025) implements revocable decoding in dLLMs, enabling the refinement of suspicious tokens. However, these decoding strate-gies are all based on semi-autoregressive decoding, where advanced decoding typically operates only within a single block per step, without leveraging already stable tokens in future blocks.

D Comparison with Other Works 

In this section, we focus on the differences be-tween AHD and other decoding methods. Although KLASS (Kim et al., 2026) also introduces the con-cept of KL divergence, it is important to emphasize that AHD is fundamentally different from the step-wise KL adopted in KLASS, which only compares adjacent distributions (i.e., pt and pt+1 ). In con-trast, AHD fixes the current distribution P tj as an anchor and retrospectively compares it against the predictions along the entire historical trajectory. On the one hand, such an anchor-based track-ing mechanism provides a more direct character-ization of whether the current prediction remains consistently stable over a period of time, rather than merely reflecting local fluctuations between two neighboring steps, thereby avoiding mislead-ing judgments caused by local fluctuations.
